Day 1: Arrival & Setup Arrive 10 PM

Take a taxi or train (15 min) from Nice Côte d'Azur Airport to Cagnes-sur-Mer.

Day 2: The Corniche Loop 102 km

102kmDist
1,850mClimb

Route: Menton - Col de Castillon - Sospel. Classic alpine coastal mix.

Download GPX
Afternoon: After the ride, head up to the stunning perched medieval village of Historic Haut-de-Cagnes — one of the best-preserved hilltop villages on the Côte d'Azur. Wander its narrow cobblestone alleys, vine-draped archways and sun-baked terracotta facades that have barely changed since the Middle Ages.
  • Climb to the Château Grimaldi (14th century), which crowns the hilltop and houses the Musée de l'Olivier (Museum of the Olive Tree) and the Musée d'Art Moderne Méditerranéen — featuring works by Renoir, Dufy and Chagall donated by artists who lived on the Riviera.
  • Don't miss the tower viewpoint — on a clear day you can see the full sweep of the Alps inland and the Mediterranean coastline from Menton to the Esterel.
  • Stroll the Place du Château and duck into the small artisan boutiques and galleries tucked into the ramparts.
  • Tip: The château is closed on Tuesdays — plan accordingly. Entry is inexpensive (around €5) and well worth it.
Dinner: Le Grimaldi sits right in the old village and is one of the most atmospheric restaurants in the area. Expect classic Provençal cuisine — daube de boeuf, fresh pasta with truffle, and a well-chosen regional wine list. Book ahead, especially in summer, as the terrace tables with views over the bay go fast. Budget around €35–50 per person for three courses.

Day 3: Gorges du Loup 115 km

115kmDist
1,650mClimb

Route: Inland via Cagnes and Vence to the spectacular Gorges and Gourdon.

Download GPX
Afternoon: After descending from the Gorges, make your way to the hilltop village of Haut-de-Cagnes and the magnificent Château-musée Grimaldi — a formidable 14th-century stronghold built by Rainier Grimaldi, later transformed by Henri Grimaldi in the 17th century into a luxurious palatial residence, and today one of the most underrated museums on the entire Riviera. Budget 1.5–2 hours to do it justice.
  • The Tower Viewpoint — Climb to the very top of the keep for unbroken 360° panoramic views: the snowy peaks of the Maritime Alps inland, and the full arc of the Mediterranean coastline stretching from the Esterel headlands in the west to Cap Ferrat in the east. One of the finest free-standing vistas on the coast.
  • Musée d'Art Moderne Méditerranéen — A surprisingly rich collection of post-Impressionist and modern works donated by artists who lived and worked on the Riviera, including pieces by Vaszary, Carzou and others. The museum rotates exhibitions regularly, so there is almost always something new to discover.
  • Musée de l'Olivier — A charming ethnographic museum dedicated to the olive tree and its central role in Provençal culture: ancient pressing tools, ceramic vessels, folklore and agricultural history. Unexpectedly moving and very local in spirit.
  • 17th-century frescoed ceilings — The ceremonial rooms of the château retain their spectacular painted ceilings, including the famous Fall of Phaeton fresco by the Genoese painter Giovanni Battista Carlone — a Baroque masterpiece rarely visited by tourists.
  • Practical tips: Closed on Tuesdays. Entry ~€6, free on the first Sunday of each month. Parking is very limited — arrive by foot from the lower village or take the small free shuttle (navette) that runs from the seafront.
Dinner: La Maison de la Pêche is the go-to address for serious fresh seafood in Cagnes-sur-Mer, sitting close to the fishing harbour at Cros-de-Cagnes. The catch changes daily depending on what the local boats bring in — expect whole grilled sea bass or sea bream (loup or daurade), prawns from the Gulf, and a perfectly simple bouillabaisse on certain evenings. The wine list leans into Provençal rosés from Bellet and Bandol. Book at least a day ahead in high season. Budget €40–55 per person for a full meal with wine.

Day 4: Esterel Red Rocks 98 km

98kmDist
900mClimb
Route: Cagnes → Cannes → Corniche d'Or → Agay → Col du Testanier → DN7 → Cagnes. Download GPX
Afternoon: After the long miles along the Corniche d'Or, the Esterel coast offers some of the most dramatic post-ride recovery spots on the Riviera. Head down to the shoreline between Agay and Anthéor, where the vivid red porphyry rock meets crystal-clear turquoise water — the colour contrast is unlike anything else on the French coast.
  • Plage d'Agay — The largest and most sheltered bay in the area, with calm, shallow water ideal for a long cool-down swim after the ride. Some sun-lounger hire available in season; the eastern end stays quieter and is free.
  • Calanque du Petit Caneiret — A tiny, secluded cove a short walk from the road, packed with smooth flat pebbles and remarkably clear water. Worth the extra 10 minutes on foot to escape the crowds.
  • Plage de la Baumette (Anthéor) — Framed by rust-red cliffs and pine trees tumbling down to the shore; one of the most photographed spots in the Esterel. Best visited late afternoon when the light turns the rocks deep orange.
  • Tip: Bring water shoes — the pebble and rock entries can be sharp. A small beach bag strapped to the bike makes the detour effortless.
Dinner: L'Atelier is one of Cagnes-sur-Mer's most exciting dining addresses — a compact, chef-driven bistro where the menu changes with the market and the seasons. The cooking style is best described as creative Provençal bistronomy: technically precise but never fussy, with bold flavour combinations rooted in local ingredients.
  • Expect dishes like agneau des Alpes with roasted aubergine and tapenade, handmade pasta with wild herbs, or a beautifully composed fish of the day from the Antibes market.
  • The desserts punch well above the price point — the tarte au citron revisitée is a standout if it appears on the board.
  • Wine list is compact but well curated: ask for the glass pairings, which change to match the daily menu. Côtes de Provence and Bellet dominate the by-the-glass selection.
  • Practical: The room is small — reserve in advance. A tasting menu option is typically available for around €45–55; à la carte lands at €30–40 per person with wine.

Day 5: The Madone Benchmark 88 km

88kmDist
2,100mClimb

Route: Cagnes - Nice - Menton - Col de la Madone - Peille. High intensity.

Download GPX
Afternoon: After the brutal Col de la Madone, descend into the Saint-Paul-de-Vence — one of the most celebrated medieval hilltop villages in all of France and a living museum of 20th-century art. The village has attracted artists since the 1920s and the atmosphere is still uniquely creative and unhurried.
  • Fondation Maeght — One of Europe's finest private modern art foundations, set in a stunning building designed by Josep Lluís Sert. The permanent collection includes Miró, Calder, Giacometti, Léger and Chagall; the sculpture garden alone is worth the entry fee (~€18). Allow 1.5–2 hours.
  • Village ramparts walk — Stroll along the 16th-century ramparts for sweeping views across the Var valley. The main street (Grande Rue) is lined with galleries, ceramic studios and artisan boutiques — quality is genuinely high compared to most tourist towns.
  • La Colombe d'Or — The legendary inn where Picasso, Matisse and Léger once settled their bar tabs with paintings. Even if you're not staying, the terrace bar is an unmissable aperitif stop. Order a glass of local Bellet rosé and soak in the atmosphere.
  • Marc Chagall's grave — In the village cemetery at the southern end; a quiet, surprisingly moving pilgrimage spot for art lovers.
  • Tip: Arrive before 4 PM to avoid coach-tour crowds. The village becomes far more peaceful in the late afternoon golden light.
Dinner: Stay in Saint-Paul or descend to Cagnes. Le Tilleul on the village square is reliably excellent for Provençal cuisine with a view — try the soupe de poisson and the lamb. Budget ~€35–50 per person.

Day 6: The Queen Stage 122 km

122kmDist
2,600mClimb

Route: The mighty Col de Turini loop. Pure mountain terrain.

Download GPX
Afternoon — Mandatory Rest Day: After the Queen Stage, your body demands full recovery. Keep it easy: a flat 20-minute walk to the Cros-de-Cagnes Promenade is all you need for today's "activity."
  • The Promenade itself — A gentle 2 km seafront walkway lined with fishing boats, beach bars and old men playing pétanque. The Cros-de-Cagnes harbour is one of the last working fishing ports between Antibes and Nice — it still smells of brine and diesel in the best possible way. Walk to the end of the jetty for a clean view back toward the Esterel massif.
  • Afternoon swim — The beach in front of the promenade is fine shingle and drops off quickly — ideal for salt-water recovery. The water here is slightly calmer than the open beaches further east.
  • Legs-up café stop — Le Sporting café terrace right on the harbour is the local favourite for a post-ride coffee and a Monaco (lager, grenadine and lemonade — the Riviera's recovery drink of choice). Watch the sun drop behind the Esterel hills.
  • Foam roll & nutrition: Use the evening to refuel properly. Prioritise carbohydrates and protein — you have two big days left. A 30-minute stretch and foam-roll session before dinner will make a meaningful difference tomorrow.
Dinner: Keep it local and easy. Bistrot de la Marine (on the harbour front) is perfect for tonight — simple grilled fish, generous portions and zero fuss. The moules marinières and frites are a solid choice for post-ride carb-loading. Budget €25–35 per person. Early to bed — ideally by 10 PM.

Day 7: Var Valley Silent Peaks 80 km

1,200mClimb

Route: Exploring the rugged, quiet roads of Gilette and Utelle.

Download GPX
Afternoon: The quiet Var Valley roads finish back near Cagnes, making Polygone Riviera a convenient and surprisingly enjoyable post-ride wind-down. This is the Riviera's largest open-air shopping and lifestyle complex — architecturally striking and far more pleasant than a typical retail park.
  • Cycling gear top-up — Several sports and outdoor retailers are on site. If you've gone through a chamois cream, gels, or need a CO₂ cartridge for the final two days, this is your best opportunity to restock without going into Nice.
  • Food court & terraces — Over 30 restaurants and casual dining spots, from Côté Sushi to artisan burger bars and Provençal bistros. The central plaza terraces are open-air and pleasant in the late afternoon. A good spot to sit with a coffee and watch the Riviera crowd go by.
  • Fnac & bookshops — Pick up a detailed IGN map of the Maritime Alps or a cycling guide to the region as a memento — far more meaningful than a generic souvenir.
  • Grocery run — There is a large Carrefour on site. Use it to stock up on race-day nutrition: rice cakes, pain au lait, Haribo, mini Babybels and the usual Riviera staple — socca flour pancakes from the deli counter if they have them.
  • Practical: 5–10 minute ride from most Cagnes accommodation; free secure bike parking at the main entrance.
Dinner: Fleur de Sel (Rue du Maréchal Joffre, Cagnes) is a refined but relaxed neighbourhood restaurant — excellent for a proper sit-down meal before the final two days. The risotto aux truffes and the pan-fried saint-pierre (John Dory) are both outstanding. Wine list strong on local appellations. Budget €40–55 per person.

Day 8: Italian Border Loop 110 km

110kmDist
1,800mClimb

Route: Across to Italy for coffee in Dolceacqua, then back via Col d'Èze.

Afternoon: After crossing back from Italy via Col d'Èze, make the short detour to Château de Crémat — one of the most extraordinary wine estates on the entire Côte d'Azur, producing the rare and prestigious Bellet AOC wines, a tiny appellation almost entirely confined to the hills above Nice.
  • Estate tour — The château dates to the early 20th century and is built in a distinctive neo-Gothic style surrounded by terraced vineyards. Guided tours of the cellars and vineyard are available (book ahead via the website). The winemaker will explain the unique geology of the Bellet hills — the poudingue (conglomerate stone) and Alpine micro-climate that make these wines unlike anything else in Provence.
  • Tasting — The estate produces red, white and rosé across several cuvées. Highlights to try: the white Bellet made from Rolle grapes (~80% Vermentino) — floral, mineral, and an ideal match for the seafood you've been eating all week — and the structured red Bellet from Braquet and Folle Noire, which is almost impossible to find outside Nice.
  • Buy to take home — Bellet wines are genuinely rare exports; the estate shop is your best chance to bring back bottles that no one at home will have tried. A mixed case of 6 is very manageable in checked luggage.
  • Practical: Open Mon–Sat, tastings from ~€15 per person. Located in the hills above Nice, about 20 minutes by bike from Cagnes via the Corniche road. Call ahead to confirm tasting availability.
Dinner: Head back into Cagnes and dine at Vivo on the seafront at Cros-de-Cagnes — a contemporary Mediterranean restaurant with excellent natural wines. The carpaccio de daurade and char-grilled octopus are house signatures. Pair with a bottle of the Bellet white you just tasted this afternoon. Budget €40–55 per person with wine.

Day 9: Final Panoramic Loop 95 km

95kmDist
1,900mClimb

Route: Col de Vence and a final steep kick up Mont Chauve. Gear check at Eat Sleep Cycle Hub.

Afternoon: The final ride ends in style. After descending from Mont Chauve, reward yourself with an afternoon in Vieux Nice — the old town is one of the most vivid and genuinely authentic urban neighbourhoods on the French Riviera, with a strong Italian Baroque character reflecting its Savoyard history.
  • Cours Saleya market — If you arrive before 5:30 PM on weekdays, the famous flower and produce market will still have traders. The afternoon session is ideal for last-minute local food souvenirs: herbes de Provence, lavender, olive oil, and jars of tapenade and anchoïade that pack easily in a jersey pocket.
  • Rue Pairolière & the old town grid — Wander the narrow ochre-painted streets, ducking into boulangeries for pan bagnat (the Niçois stuffed bread), and look up at the ornate Baroque church facades — Chapelle de la Miséricorde and the Cathédrale Sainte-Réparate are both worth a few minutes inside.
  • Castle Hill (Colline du Château) — Take the free lift from the seafront end of Vieux Nice up to the hilltop park for a final panoramic view over the Baie des Anges and the rooftops of the old town. One of the great free viewpoints in Europe. The waterfall is a nice surprise.
  • Souvenir shopping picks — Beyond food, look for hand-painted faïence ceramics (the workshop on Rue du Collet is excellent), linen printed with Niçois patterns, and a bottle of Pastis Henri Bardouin from any cave à vins.
  • Tip: Lock the bike securely at one of the stands near Place Masséna and walk in — the old town lanes are too narrow to ride and are pedestrianised.
Final Dinner: Le Charlot on the Cagnes seafront is the ideal final-night venue — unpretentious, generous and properly seafood-focused with a warm terrace facing the sea. Order the plateau de fruits de mer for the table, raise a glass of champagne to 1,000 km of Riviera roads, and remind yourself it's worth coming back. Budget €45–65 per person for the full celebratory spread.
